# AWS Organizations — Best Practices

## Common scenarios
- Centrally governing a multi-account AWS environment        → Security, Operational Excellence
- Enforcing organization-wide guardrails and compliance        → Security, Operational Excellence
- Consolidating billing and cost tracking across many accounts        → Cost Optimization
- Standardizing backups, tagging, and configuration at scale        → Reliability, Operational Excellence

## 🔒 Security
- **[account structure]** Manage all your AWS accounts within a single organization to maintain a consistent security boundary — a single organization lets you apply consistent policies, central visibility, and programmatic controls across your environment. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_best-practices.html)
- **[root user]** Enable centralized root access management to remove and monitor root user credentials for member accounts — this makes newly created member accounts secure-by-default and eliminates standing root credentials as an attack surface. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_best-practices.html)
- **[OU design]** Group accounts into organizational units based on business purpose and common control requirements, not reporting structure — start with foundational Security and Infrastructure OUs, each split into production and non-production, so policies can be applied consistently at the OU level. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_manage_ous_best_practices.html)
- **[SCPs]** Use service control policies as preventive guardrails to set the maximum available permissions for accounts, OUs, or the whole organization — SCPs never grant permissions themselves, so they must be paired with identity- or resource-based policies that actually grant access. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/security-reference-architecture/organizations-security.html)
- **[SCPs]** Test SCP changes with the IAM policy simulator and review IAM service-last-accessed data and CloudTrail usage before rolling out new or modified SCPs — SCP changes take effect immediately across every affected account, so understanding blast radius up front avoids breaking legitimate workloads. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/security-reference-architecture/organizations-security.html)
- **[SCPs]** Stage and validate new SCPs in a dedicated policy-staging OU or at the individual-account level before attaching them broadly to the organization — this limits the risk of a bad policy affecting hundreds of accounts simultaneously. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_manage_ous_best_practices.html)
- **[RCPs]** Use resource control policies alongside SCPs when you need to restrict or prevent external identities from accessing resources inside your organization — SCPs govern what your principals can do, while RCPs govern what any principal (including external ones) can do to your resources. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/security-reference-architecture/organizations-security.html)
- **[declarative policies]** Use declarative policies to enforce durable, organization-wide baseline configuration (for example, blocking public internet access to VPC resources) at the AWS service control-plane level — unlike authorization policies, these keep the desired configuration in place even as new accounts, principals, or service features appear. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/security-reference-architecture/organizations-security.html)
- **[management account]** Avoid running workloads in the management account and reserve it for essential administrative tasks only — SCPs and RCPs do not apply to the management account, so keeping it workload-free reduces the account's exposure. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/prescriptive-guidance/latest/security-reference-architecture/organizations-security.html)
- **[delegated administration]** Register dedicated delegated administrator accounts for security and management services (for example AWS Config) instead of operating them from the management account — this enforces separation of duties and avoids granting broad teams access to the organization's most privileged account. [doc](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/mt/using-delegated-admin-for-aws-config-operations-and-aggregation/)
- **[account recovery]** Keep the account's contact phone number and a business-managed group email address current for the root user of every account — these are the recovery paths AWS uses to verify account ownership, and a group email avoids single-person dependency during incident response. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_best-practices.html)
- **[SCP scope]** Design SCPs for clear-cut, either/or security controls (such as denying access outside approved AWS Regions) rather than fine-grained business logic — SCPs apply organization-wide and even the root user in a member account cannot override them, so they are best suited to hard security boundaries. [doc](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/publicsector/data-security-governance-best-practices-education-state-local-government/)

## 🛡️ Reliability
- **[isolation]** Use multiple accounts to isolate workloads, distribute service quotas, and separate production from non-production environments — this contains the blast radius of an incident or misconfiguration to a single account or OU rather than the whole estate. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_best-practices.html)
- **[backup policies]** Centrally manage AWS Backup plans across the organization using backup policies attached to the root, OUs, or accounts — the inherited effective policy standardizes backup rules, retention, and cross-account/cross-Region copies, reducing the risk of accounts drifting out of compliance. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_manage_policies_backup.html)
- **[backup monitoring]** Enable cross-account monitoring for AWS Backup once trusted access is turned on for the organization — this gives visibility into backup, copy, and restore job health across all accounts from a single place. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/aws-backup/latest/devguide/whatisbackup.html)

## ⚙️ Operational Excellence
- **[OU structure]** Build foundational OUs for Security and Infrastructure first, then add Sandbox, Workloads, Policy Staging, Suspended, and Exceptions OUs as the environment matures — this gives every account a clear home with a well-understood, common set of controls. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_manage_ous_best_practices.html)
- **[suspended accounts]** Attach an SCP that denies all actions to a dedicated Suspended OU for closed accounts awaiting deletion — tagging them for traceability keeps a clear audit trail in case they need to be restored. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_manage_ous_best_practices.html)
- **[service enablement]** Enable or disable AWS service integrations with Organizations through each service's own console, API, or CLI operations rather than generic account settings — this lets the service perform its required initialization and cleanup steps correctly. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_best-practices.html)
- **[delegated administration]** Register delegated administrator accounts for services such as AWS Config — this lets security, audit, and compliance teams aggregate organization-wide data without needing access to the management account. [doc](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/mt/using-delegated-admin-for-aws-config-operations-and-aggregation/)
- **[tagging]** Define an organization-wide tagging strategy and enforce it with tag policies so that tags stay consistent across accounts — tag policies can prevent noncompliant tagging operations on supported resource types, which keeps automation, cost allocation, and access control (ABAC) reliable at scale. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_manage_policies_tag-policies.html)
- **[tagging + SCPs]** Combine tag policies with service control policies to both standardize tag values and deny resource creation or tag deletion that doesn't comply — this closes the gap between reporting tags as noncompliant and actually preventing noncompliant operations. [doc](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/mt/implement-aws-resource-tagging-strategy-using-aws-tag-policies-and-service-control-policies-scps/)
- **[policy delegation]** Where appropriate, delegate management of specific policy types (for example tag policies) to a trusted member account rather than concentrating all policy administration in the management account — this reduces reliance on management-account credentials for routine governance work. [doc](https://aws.amazon.com/blogs/mt/delegate-aws-organizations-policy-management-in-a-multi-account-environment/)

## 💰 Cost Optimization
- **[account structure]** Implement an account structure with a management account and member accounts grouped into OUs by business unit, environment, or workload — this gives clean cost and usage boundaries while consolidated billing still aggregates usage for volume pricing. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/wellarchitected/latest/cost-optimization-pillar/cost_govern_usage_account_structure.html)
- **[consolidated billing]** Use the consolidated billing feature so all member-account charges roll up into a single bill paid by the management account — this combines usage across accounts to share volume discounts, Reserved Instance discounts, and Savings Plans, typically lowering overall spend versus standalone accounts. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/cost-management/latest/userguide/what-is-costmanagement.html)
- **[cost visibility]** Grant business users read-only access to billing and cost tools (such as a restricted permission set for Cost Explorer) instead of broad management-account access — this provides cost visibility without granting unnecessary privileges. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/organizations/latest/userguide/orgs_best-practices.html)
- **[workload placement]** Keep all workload resources in member accounts and none in the management account — this keeps cost attribution clean per account/OU and avoids mixing administrative and workload spend. [doc](https://docs.aws.amazon.com/wellarchitected/latest/cost-optimization-pillar/cost_govern_usage_account_structure.html)
